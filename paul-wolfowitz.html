Take a Look at Paul Wolfowitz's Career

In 1977, he began working at the Arms Control and Disarmament Agency in the US government, where he served as a special assistant to the director.

In 1981, Wolfowitz was appointed as the head of policy planning at the US Department of State, where he advised then-Secretary of State Alexander Haig on a range of foreign policy issues. He also served as the US ambassador to Indonesia from 1986 to 1989.

From 1989 to 1993, Wolfowitz served as the undersecretary of defence for policy in the George H.W. Bush administration. In this role, he was a key figure in shaping US defence policy in the post-Cold War era.

 Wolfowitz returned to academia in 1993, when he became the dean of the Paul H. Nitze School of Advanced International Studies (SAIS) at Johns Hopkins University. He remained in this position until 2001 when he was appointed as the deputy secretary of defence in the George W. Bush administration.

During his time as deputy secretary of defence, Wolfowitz was a leading advocate for the 2003 invasion of Iraq. He also played a key role in the US response to the September 11th attacks and the subsequent War on Terror.

In 2005, Wolfowitz was appointed as the president of the World Bank, a position he held until 2007. His tenure at the World Bank was controversial, as he was accused of using his position to benefit his then-girlfriend, Shaha Riza, by arranging a high-paying job for her at the State Department.

After leaving the World Bank, Wolfowitz returned to academia, where he held positions at the American Enterprise Institute, the Johns Hopkins School of Advanced International Studies, and the School of International and Public Affairs at Columbia University.
